The disadvantages Syracuse City School District works around every day

Inside one of New York's poorest school districts, and alternatives the district must take to give students a chance

By Matt Streicher, S.I. Newhouse School of Public Communications

SYRACUSE, N.Y. — New York State Public Schools currently enroll more than 2.4 million students state-wide. The system is the fourth largest in the country behind only California, Texas, and Florida.

Of the 2.4 million students enrolled in NY public schools, over 60% fall into the category of “economically disadvantaged”. This category includes all students who participate or whose family members participate in economic assistance programs like free or reduced-price lunch programs, SNAP benefits, and others.

By this definition, over 1.4 million students are economically disadvantaged. The vast majority of those students are from New York City public schools simply because of the sheer volume of students in those areas. In Upstate NY, there are fewer students per district, but the same trends are prevalent in some places like Onondaga County.

Onondaga county itself shows rates of disadvantaged students that are reasonably consistent with the rest of the state, but when the magnifying glass focuses on Syracuse City School District, it gets completely disproportionate. 82% of students enrolled in SCSD are economically disadvantaged. This makes SCSD the district with the 4th highest child poverty rate among 648 districts in New York State.

Economic disadvantage affects almost every other school statistic tracked by New York State Education Department. 24% of 3rd-8th graders in SCSD are categorized as proficient in English language arts, and 18% are proficient in math. Scores on statewide tests are low, and the district relies heavily on state funding.

The school district has been well aware of these issues and is faced with finding ways to bring test scores up while minimizing economic strain. They introduced the PL program (Personalized Learning) across the district which focuses on adaptive instruction that can shift to meet the needs of each student.

Laura Kelley, Chief of Student Support Services for Syracuse City School District, works with students every day to ensure they get the support they need in order to succeed in school and beyond. She said the personalized learning program is part of how SCSD is working with what they and their students have access to.

“Personalized learning is how we ensure that every student has a chance to learn in their own way. Understanding what they need to do their best is a top priority of every one of our faculty.” said Kelley.

The district also incentivizes higher scores through the Syracuse University Challenge, which was developed by SCSD and Syracuse University. A student enters into an agreement with the school district in the 8th grade and agrees to earn a NY State Regents Diploma with advanced designation, maintain an 85% average for each year in high school, and achieve an SAT combined score of at least 1100 or ACT composite score of at least 23. Students who complete the requirements and fulfill the agreement are guaranteed admission into the College of Arts & Sciences at SU. Students are also provided an individualized financial package to meet the specific needs of each family.

Kelley said that meeting the students where they are and guaranteeing them a path after graduation, dependent on grades, is part of the district’s responsibility to set good students up for success and create a new standard for the rest of the district.

“We think the SU challenge helps show our students that grades can lead to success no matter who you are or where you come from. Financial stability being a part of the agreement is how we make the challenge accessible to all of our students, and that comes back to our focus on giving students what they need."

Graduation rates for SCSD are a bright spot for the district recently. The class graduating in the summer of 2015 had a grad rate of just under 50%. Then, it slowly began to climb. After COVID, the SCSD graduation rate hit an all-time high of 77.2%, and has stayed above 70% every year since 2020 except 2023 (69%).

Kelley emphasized that the graduation rate rising is an accomplishment not just for the district, but for the students as well.

“The graduation rates recently are something we want to build on. We’re seeing the kids have a real desire to get their diploma not just for their parents, but for themselves, and that’s what helped get the rates up.”

Even though disadvantaged, Syracuse City School Districts are not complacent in the performance of their students. Improving the economic status of the students and community as a whole is the long-term goal, but now, the district is doing everything it can to meet the students where they are in how they teach and interact with them every day. The Personalized Learning program, SU Challenge, and how the teachers adapt their approach all create an environment that focuses on giving students what they need and shows what can happen with good effort and positive attitude.
